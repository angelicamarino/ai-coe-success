01 · The Strategic Moment

The risk isn't that UNOPS
won't build AI.

The risk is that UNOPS will build it and fail to embed it. Across the organization, enthusiasm for AI is real and growing. Without a coordination center that earns genuine trust quickly, that enthusiasm fragments into competing initiatives, inconsistent choices, and tools that get launched but never truly land.

The window
AI initiatives are already forming across business units and field operations, some coordinated, many not. The CoE has a narrow window to establish itself as the natural home for these conversations before the landscape calcifies. Waiting compounds the problem. Every month without a functioning CoE is a month in which AI adoption happens around it rather than through it.
The real gap
The gap in UNOPS today is not the absence of AI products. It is the absence of last-mile capability: the organizational muscle to take something built and get it genuinely adopted. A tool that sits unused is not an asset. It is a liability that erodes confidence in the next initiative.
What this means
Success for the CoE is not measured in use cases collected or tools catalogued. It is measured in adoption depth, whether AI products are genuinely changing how people work, at scale, across a decentralized global organization. That is a harder problem than building the tools. It requires different skills, and deliberate focus.
The pace question
Speed is a legitimate concern. But speed without landing is just noise. The CoE's job is not to slow things down. It is to ensure that what gets built actually sticks. Capability building and solution delivery are not sequential. Done well, they reinforce each other: people learn AI by using real tools on real problems, not in advance of them.
02 · What Success Looks Like

Three directives.
One coherent test.

The charter defines three directives. Success across all three can be tested against a single question: is the CoE the place the organization comes to, or the place it routes around?

Directive 01 · Capability & Adoption Accelerator
Build Capability
Getting the organization genuinely AI-literate, not through abstract training, but through capability built in the context of real tools and real work. Literacy that sticks is always embedded in doing.
What success looks like
Proactive engagementTeams come to the CoE before launching AI initiatives, not after.
Embedded literacyPeople understand AI well enough to ask better questions of IT and push back when a solution doesn't fit the problem.
Field reachCapability building isn't headquarter-centric. Field offices are engaged and reflected in training design.
How it gets measured
Adoption depthAre tools being used 60 days after launch, not just in week one?
Inbound volumeIs the CoE receiving more requests over time, without being mandated?
Stakeholder perceptionDo business unit leaders describe the CoE as useful, not bureaucratic?
What the CoE owns
The narrativeTranslating what IT builds into language that moves people to act.
Training designLearning wrapped around real products, not standalone modules.
Early adopter strategyKnowing which teams to activate first and in what sequence.
Directive 02 · Solution Incubator
Build Solutions
Identifying, prioritizing, and getting the right AI solutions into the hands of people who will actually use them. The hard part is not building. It is choosing what to build and ensuring it lands.
What success looks like
Fewer, better choicesA small portfolio of high-impact solutions with clear owners, not a long list of use cases without momentum.
Time to launchProducts move from ready to landed without stalling in a gap between IT and the organization.
Demonstrable valueAt least two or three solutions in year one with documented, measurable business impact.
How it gets measured
Launch velocityTime between product readiness and organizational rollout.
Use case qualityProportion of scoped use cases that reach deployment, not just the pipeline size.
Business outcome attributionEven directional evidence that a solution changed a process or decision.
What the CoE owns
Prioritization judgmentAssessing which problems are worth solving with AI and how much investment they warrant.
The last mileEverything between a finished product and genuine organizational adoption.
Product feedback loopStructured insight from users back to IT to improve what has been built.
Directive 03 · Partnerships & Outreach Hub
Build Partnerships
Positioning UNOPS as a credible and trusted actor in the AI space, externally with partners and donors, internally across a decentralized organization that operates with significant regional autonomy.
What success looks like
External credibilityPeer organizations and donors view UNOPS as a serious, responsible AI actor, not a follower.
Internal alignmentField offices and regions see the CoE as a resource, not a headquarters imposition.
Idea flowReal-world problems from partners and field operations are informing what the CoE prioritizes, not just internal IT ambition.
How it gets measured
Partner engagementNumber of substantive external conversations that generate actionable intelligence for the CoE.
Field representationWhether the portfolio of solutions reflects needs from outside HQ.
Inbound interestExternal parties seeking UNOPS out for AI collaboration, not the reverse.
What the CoE owns
The UNOPS AI narrativeA coherent external story about what UNOPS is doing and why it matters.
Field engagement strategyHow the CoE reaches into decentralized operations without feeling top-down.
Problem sourcingActively translating partner and field needs into the CoE's solution pipeline.
03 · What the Role Demands

More sophisticated
than it first appears.

The Coordinator is the operational engine of the CoE, not a support function. In the early phase, the success of the CoE and the performance of this role are essentially the same thing.

01 Last-Mile Ownership

The gap between a product being technically ready and an organization genuinely adopting it is wide and almost never filled automatically. The Coordinator owns this gap entirely. This means the rollout narrative, the training design, the sequencing of who gets access when, the identification of champions, and the feedback loop back to IT.

This is not a communications task. It is a strategic and political challenge that requires knowing the organization well enough to anticipate resistance, identify the right first movers, and build momentum that sustains beyond week one.

⚠ The signal to watch: a product that has been "ready to launch" for weeks without launching is a last-mile failure, not a technical one.
02 Prioritization Judgment

A long list of use cases is not a strategy. The Coordinator must be able to assess the value of a problem before reaching for a solution, understanding whether AI is the right tool, how complex a solution needs to be, and how much organizational energy is worth investing.

This judgment is rarer than it sounds. It requires enough technical fluency to evaluate feasibility, enough business understanding to assess value, and enough organizational knowledge to anticipate adoption risk. Collecting use cases is easy. Knowing which three to act on first is the skill.

⚠ The signal to watch: a pipeline full of use cases with no clear prioritization rationale and no active deployments.
03 Technical Fluency as a Bridge

The Coordinator does not need to be an engineer. But they need to be able to sit in a product design conversation and push back intelligently, to recognize when a proposed solution is more complex than the problem warrants, or when a feature will create friction rather than remove it.

They also need to bring ideas forward, to see a problem in the organization and translate it into a product concept that IT can act on. This requires comfort with AI capabilities: understanding what is genuinely easy versus hard to build, what works reliably versus what is still immature, and how to describe a business need in terms a technical team can work with.

04 Organizational Navigation

UNOPS is a decentralized, globally distributed organization. The Coordinator needs to earn trust from people who did not ask for a CoE and make the CoE feel relevant to field operations, not just a headquarters initiative. This is fundamentally a relationship job, and relationships take time that an external hire simply does not have.

Knowing which business unit leaders are natural champions, which will be skeptical, and how to engage each of them differently is not something that can be learned from an onboarding document. It comes from having worked in the organization, from knowing the politics, the informal networks, and the history of past digital initiatives.

05 Governance Without Friction

The CoE is responsible for governance, but governance done badly is the fastest way to make the CoE irrelevant. The Coordinator must design processes that feel like guidance, not gatekeeping. This means lightweight, fast, and clearly useful, so that teams choose to engage rather than route around it.

The charter mandates that all organization-wide AI initiatives follow CoE-led governance. Making that real without creating a bottleneck requires significant judgment about when to be firm and when to enable, and the credibility to hold that line with both IT and business stakeholders.

04 · Why Internal Is the Only Viable Path

Organizational knowledge
is not a preference.

It is the prerequisite. Given the pace the organization needs to move at, the cost of an external hire spending their first six months learning the landscape is a cost UNOPS cannot afford to pay. Internal appointment is the strategic choice, not the comfortable one.

The external hire risk
What gets lost without organizational context
  • Months spent mapping who to talk to, who holds informal influence, and how decisions actually get made
  • No existing relationships with business unit leaders. Trust must be built from zero while simultaneously delivering
  • No understanding of the existing IT product landscape, past digital initiatives, or why certain approaches have succeeded or failed
  • Governance processes designed without understanding how the organization actually functions, likely to create friction rather than remove it
  • Field and regional dynamics entirely opaque, leading to a headquarters-centric CoE by default
The internal advantage
What organizational knowledge actually enables
  • Able to identify the right early adopters and champions from day one, no discovery phase required
  • Existing credibility with stakeholders across functions. The CoE earns trust faster because the Coordinator already has it
  • Understands the IT product landscape and can immediately connect business needs to existing capabilities
  • Knows the informal networks and can navigate resistance before it becomes visible
  • Can design governance that fits how UNOPS actually operates, not how an org chart says it should
The bottom line
"Waiting a year to fill this role is not a neutral decision. It is a choice to let AI adoption continue without coordination, and the cost of that compounds monthly."
The right internal candidate, someone with the organizational knowledge, the technical fluency to engage credibly with IT, the judgment to prioritize well, and the capability to own the last mile, is more valuable to this role than any external hire with a stronger AI résumé. The combination of skills required here is only found by someone who already knows what they are walking into.
